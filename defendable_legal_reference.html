The Extraordinary Circumstances Test

The fundamental defence under Article 5(3). Both limbs must be satisfied cumulatively. Interpreted strictly as a derogation from the consumer-protection purpose of the Regulation. A third requirement — avoidability — operates independently.

Limb 1 — Not Inherent
The event must, by its nature or origin, not be inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of an air carrier.
Limb 2 — Beyond Control
The event must be beyond the carrier's actual control on account of its nature or origin.
Third requirement — avoidability: Even where extraordinary circumstances are proved, the carrier must show the delay "could not have been avoided even if all reasonable measures had been taken." This is assessed separately and is the point most defences actually fail on.

50% reduction: Available where carrier offers re-routing and passenger arrives within the applicable time window of scheduled arrival. Rarely applicable where flight already arrives 3hrs+ late. Assessed against scheduled, not actual, arrival time of re-routing flight (C-451/20).
Sturgeon principle: A 3-hour arrival delay at final destination triggers the same compensation right as cancellation. Sturgeon v Condor C-402/07 (2009); confirmed Grand Chamber Nelson/TUI Travel C-581/10, C-629/10 (2012).
Article 9 — Right to Care
McDonagh principle: Art 9 care (meals, hotel, transport) is absolute — owed even where extraordinary circumstances are proved and Art 7 compensation is not payable. No temporal or monetary limit exists in current law. McDonagh v Ryanair C-12/11 (2013).

Technical Defects — General Rule
Default position: NOT extraordinary. Technical defects are inherent in the normal exercise of airline operations. This position is settled and binding in both EU and UK jurisdictions.
NOT Extraordinary
Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia
C-549/07 · ECJ · 22 December 2008
Complex engine turbine failure not extraordinary. Exception only where hidden manufacturing defect confirmed by OEM or competent authority affecting all aircraft of that type.
TechnicalLeading authority
NOT Extraordinary
Van der Lans v KLM
C-257/14 · ECJ · 17 September 2015
Engine component failure before average lifetime, undetected at routine maintenance. Still not extraordinary. Unexpected technical faults are inherent unless stem from external event beyond carrier's control.

Hidden Design Defects — 2024 CJEU Rulings
Exception to the technical rule: Two June 2024 CJEU judgments confirmed that hidden manufacturing design defects can constitute extraordinary circumstances — even where the carrier was aware of the defect before the disruption.
Extraordinary
Matkustaja A v Finnair
C-385/23 · ECJ · 13 June 2024
Fuel gauge design defect in aircraft in service ~5 months. EC established where: (1) unexpected defect in new aircraft model; (2) manufacturer subsequently confirms it is a hidden design defect; (3) affects all aircraft of that type; (4) implicates flight safety.
Design defect2024
Extraordinary
D S.A. v P S.A. (engine defect)
C-411/23 · ECJ · 13 June 2024
Hidden engine design defect in high-pressure compressor blades. Carrier knew of defect months before. CJEU: timing of awareness is irrelevant. Defect = extraordinary if it existed at time of disruption and was beyond carrier's control. Backup fleet not always required — court must assess whether charter (dry/wet lease) was feasible given carrier's resources.
Design defectReasonable measures2024
What evidence is required: OEM or competent authority (EASA/national CAA) confirmation that: the defect is a design issue (not maintenance); affects the aircraft type generally; implicates flight safety. An EASA Emergency Airworthiness Directive is the strongest evidence. A manufacturer service bulletin alone may not suffice.
Fume Events — bleed-air contamination ("smelly sock" events)
Default position: NOT extraordinary. A fume event — oil-contaminated bleed air entering the cabin, typically smelling of dirty socks, hot lubricant or musty air — is a technical event. Under the settled Wallentin-Hermann / Van der Lans line, technical faults are inherent to airline operations and the carrier must show more than "unexpected component failure" to escape compensation.
Why this area is a graveyard for airline defences. Most reported fume events stem from oil-seal deterioration, hydraulic leaks, bleed-valve wear or improper maintenance — all of which the CJEU has repeatedly held are inside the carrier's risk sphere. Defence attempts framed as "unforeseen technical event" have been routinely rejected in UK county courts and German Amtsgerichten. The airline loses the compensation claim (Art 7) even where the flight was correctly and safely handled.
NOT Extraordinary — Default
Van der Lans v KLM (applied by analogy)
C-257/14 · ECJ · 17 September 2015
Premature failure of an aircraft component — even undetected during routine maintenance and even where a replacement part is unavailable at the airport — is inherent to airline operations. Applied to bleed-air components (valves, seals, HP check valves) this means: a fume event caused by wear, seal deterioration or leak is NOT extraordinary in itself.
Technical faultApplies to most fume events
Extraordinary — Narrow Exception
C-385/23 Finnair · C-411/23 D.S.A. v P.S.A.
ECJ · 13 June 2024 (both)
Where the fume event stems from a hidden design defect confirmed by the OEM or by EASA/CAA, and the defect affects the aircraft type generally and implicates flight safety, the extraordinary circumstances defence CAN apply. Timing of the carrier's awareness is irrelevant (C-411/23).
Design defect routeOEM/EASA confirmation required
The only defensible route for a fume-event case. Reframe from "technical fault" to "design defect in the bleed-air system", and evidence it as such:
 
 1. Identify the specific component that leaked (bleed-air valve, engine oil seal, HP check valve, precooler seal). Tie it to a documented service bulletin, MOR (mandatory occurrence report) or maintenance finding on the specific tail.
 2. Obtain OEM or EASA written confirmation that the fault is a design/manufacturing issue affecting the aircraft type — not a maintenance failure. This is the decisive evidence under C-385/23 and C-411/23. An Emergency Airworthiness Directive is the gold standard (e.g. FAA/EASA ADs on Boeing 737 5th-stage bleed check valves; EASA A320neo HP valve butterfly seal retention clip AD 2026-0134; A330neo HP valve leaks post-Aug 2022).
 3. Show the defect implicates flight safety — for bleed-air contamination this is automatic (crew fume exposure, cabin air quality, potential incapacitation).
 4. Reasonable measures — file the MOR, document the post-event engineering inspection, justify the timeline of any diversion / return / hold, and log Art 9 care.
What will NOT hold as a defence.
• Bare assertion that the fault was "unexpected" — Van der Lans forecloses this.
• "Component reached end of life sooner than predicted" — component life is the carrier's maintenance risk.
• Reference to a service bulletin without proof the specific fault stemmed from the identified design issue.
• Reliance on manufacturer confirmation that arrives after the response deadline — obtain it before rejecting the claim (though its late arrival does not extinguish the defence per C-411/23).
Wider litigation context — DO NOT confuse. A live UK collective action (High Court, ~220 pilots and cabin crew from BA, easyJet, Jet2 and Virgin Atlantic; lead trials 2025–26) alleges long-term crew injury from bleed-air contamination — "aerotoxic syndrome". This is a personal injury / employer liability action, not an EC261 defence. It should not be cited in a passenger claim response. But the existence of a coordinated toxic-fume tort action means any airline correspondence must be handled with reputational and disclosure awareness — nothing in a passenger LOR should undermine the airline's position in the parallel PI proceedings.
Fact patternLikely outcome
Cabin fumes reported, engineering finds worn oil seal; no OEM statementPay — settle
Cabin fumes, MOR filed, OEM confirms specific defect in that component designDefend — subject to EASA/CAA confirmation on file
Cabin fumes on aircraft type subject to active EASA Emergency AD for that systemDefend — cite the AD as the evidence pack anchor
Diversion following fume event where cause is not yet identifiedJudgment call — hold classification pending engineering root cause; file Art 9 care regardless
Fume event on new aircraft (in-service <12 months) with fleet-wide occurrenceDefend — C-385/23 line applies most cleanly
Bottom line for the practitioner. Assume a fume-event Art 7 claim is payable unless the airline has (a) identified the specific bleed-air component that failed, (b) a written OEM or EASA statement that the failure is a design issue affecting the type, and (c) a clean reasonable-measures record (MOR, inspection, timeline, care). Without those three, the CJEU case law makes the defence unwinnable — this is a category where airlines lose more Art 7 claims than any other technical category because they run "unexpected technical fault" arguments the Court has expressly rejected.

ATC / Airport
Causal Chain Critical
NI, HZ v European Air Charter AG
T-656/24 · General Court · 21 January 2026
Security staff shortage at Cologne-Bonn caused first rotation delay. Carrier VOLUNTARILY waited for delayed passengers. Court: voluntary decision to wait broke the causal chain. Carrier's choice became the determining cause of subsequent delay. Full facts: third flight in the rotation arrived 3hrs+ late; carrier chartered replacement aircraft for rotations 2 and 3. Yet EC defence lost because the initial decision to wait was not obligatory.
Causal chain broken2026
The causal chain trap (21 January 2026): A carrier's voluntary operational decision — even a well-intentioned one — made after the extraordinary circumstance can become the "determining cause" of the subsequent delay, breaking the Art 5(3) defence. Airlines must carefully document whether post-EC decisions were operationally required or discretionary.

Own-Staff Industrial Action
NOT extraordinary — absolute rule. Strikes by the airline's own pilots, cabin crew or ground staff are inherent to the normal exercise of airline activity. The rule holds regardless of whether the strike is called with statutory notice or unofficially (wildcat).
NOT Extraordinary
Airhelp v Scandinavian Airlines System
C-28/20 · ECJ · 23 March 2021
SAS pilots' strike over collective-bargaining terms. Held: an own-staff strike, even one lawfully called, is part of the carrier's normal activity — Art 7 compensation is payable.
Own-staff strikeDefinitive
NOT Extraordinary
Krüsemann v TUIfly
C-195/17 · ECJ · 17 April 2018
Wildcat "sick-out" following restructuring announcement. Held: an unofficial or spontaneous own-staff action is still not extraordinary. Compensation payable across all affected flights in the cascade.
Own-staff strike
Do not run this defence. The CJEU treats industrial-relations risk as inherent to airline operation. Attempts to distinguish on the ground of statutory legitimacy, union tactics or scale have all failed. Cost-effective route is to pay quickly.
Medical Emergency / Diversion
Extraordinary — subject to evidence. A passenger medical emergency requiring the commander to divert is not inherent in airline operations and is beyond the carrier's control. The commander's diversion duty is grounded in the Air Navigation Order (UK) and ICAO standards.
Extraordinary — analogous to LE v TAP
Position by analogy to LE v Transportes Aéreos Portugueses
C-74/19 · ECJ · 11 June 2020
No CJEU judgment directly on passenger medical diversion, but the position follows a fortiori from LE v TAP — if unruly passenger conduct requiring commander diversion is extraordinary, so is a genuine medical emergency. The reasonable-measures analysis mirrors LE v TAP: the diversion is extraordinary; the timeline afterwards must be justified.
Medical diversionBy analogy
Evidence pack. Commander's report · MedLink (or equivalent physician-on-call service) log · diversion airport ground-handling notification · medical crew report at diversion airport · operational timeline for the sector and any cascade. Art 9 care survives the defence (McDonagh) — meals, hotel and transport must be provided for affected passengers.
Ground Damage
Fact-dependent — treat as technical unless third-party origin. Where the damage is caused by a third party (ground handler tug, airport vehicle, FOD on the runway, hail while parked), the Moens analogy runs and the defence may hold. Where the damage arises from the carrier's own activity, Van der Lans forecloses it.

Airport / Infrastructure Closure
Extraordinary. Airport closures, runway closures, and infrastructure failures imposed by the airport operator or a competent authority are events beyond the carrier's control and not inherent to its operation. But the reasonable-measures test remains active — the carrier must show it acted to mitigate.

C-159/18 · ECJ · 26 June 2019
Runway closure following third-party fuel spillage on the take-off runway. Held: extraordinary — provided the spillage did not originate from the carrier's own aircraft. If it had, the position reverses.

Natural Disaster
Extraordinary — within Recital 14. Volcanic eruption, earthquake, tsunami and extreme weather amounting to a natural disaster all qualify. The Art 7 defence is straightforward; the McDonagh Art 9 care duty is where airlines get caught out.
Extraordinary — but Art 9 duty absolute
Denise McDonagh v Ryanair
C-12/11 · ECJ · 31 January 2013
Icelandic Eyjafjallajökull volcanic ash cloud, week-long stranding. Held: extraordinary (indeed AG opinion called it "super-extraordinary"). But Ryanair had failed its Art 9 duty to provide meals and hotel accommodation — that duty is absolute and continues throughout the disruption, however extraordinary. Passenger's costs awarded.
Natural disasterArt 9 duty absolute
Reform 2027 note. The EC261 reform (agreed 15 June 2026, in force ~2027) introduces a three-night hotel cap for EU-departing extraordinary-circumstance cases. This will not automatically apply under UK261.
Evidence pack. Government / national civil aviation authority notice · VAAC (Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre) report for ash-cloud events · meteorological hazard forecasts · NOTAM references · Art 9 care records with receipts.
Political Unrest / War Zone
Extraordinary — Recital 14 direct. "Political instability" is expressly named in the Recital 14 list. Airspace closures imposed by governments, sanctions rendering routes unlawful, and armed conflict all qualify.
Modern context — Ukraine / Russia. Ukrainian civilian airspace has been closed continuously since 24 February 2022 (six FIRs: UKLV, UKBV, UKDV, UKFV, UKOV, UKBU). Reciprocal Russian airspace bans affect ~35 countries. Polish courts have confirmed the war-cancellation defence in EC261 proceedings connected to the Ukraine conflict. The controlling regulatory anchor is EASA CZIB 2022-01 and its revisions.

Crew Duty Limit (FDP)
Not independently extraordinary — depends on the upstream cause. A crew FDP breach is a symptom, not a cause. The extraordinary-circumstance analysis attaches to whatever caused the delay that consumed the crew's duty period.
Reserve-time duty controls
Eglītis v Latvijas Republikas Ekonomikas Ministrija
C-294/10 · ECJ · 12 May 2011
The carrier must, at the stage of organising the flight, reasonably take account of the risk of delay from possible extraordinary circumstances, and must provide for a reserve of time to allow the flight to be completed once the extraordinary circumstance has ended. Where a FDP breach could have been avoided by adequate standby crew or a schedule buffer, the reasonable-measures test fails — regardless of whether the upstream event was itself extraordinary.
Reserve-time / buffer dutyApplies downstream of every EC event
Fact patternOutcome
Weather diversion + FDP expiry, standby activated but unavailable at diversion airportDefend — buffer duty satisfied
FDP breach from routine operational delay, no evidence of standby planningPay — Eglītis test failed
ATC ground stop + FDP expiry, industry-standard standby cover in placeDefend
Overnight cancellation for FDP with no standby cover at allPay — buffer duty failed at planning stage
Evidence pack. Crew roster records with duty times · standby crew availability records for the day of disruption · FDP calculation showing the specific breach · evidence of standby activation attempts · documentation that industry-standard cover was in place at the planning stage.
Causal Chain — Breaking the Defence

Even where extraordinary circumstances are established, a carrier's voluntary operational decision made after the event can break the causal chain and expose them to liability on subsequent flights.

21 January 2026 rule (T-656/24): A carrier's autonomous operational decision — including waiting for delayed passengers — can constitute the "determining cause" of a subsequent delay if the carrier was not legally obliged to make that choice. The fact that the original EC was a necessary condition (sine qua non) for the delay is not sufficient if the carrier's own decision intervened as the determining cause.

EC261 Reform — Agreed 15 June 2026

Political agreement reached in trilogue on 15 June 2026. Formal adoption expected late 2026; entry into force approximately 2027. Does not change compensation amounts or the 3-hour threshold.

New deadline
4-day proactive passenger contact
Airline must contact disrupted passengers within 4 days to explain how to claim.
New deadline
30-day Art 7 response
Acknowledge and pay (or refuse with reasons) Art 7 compensation requests within 30 days.
New obligation
3-hour re-routing offer window
Where passenger chooses re-routing, must be offered an alternative within 3hrs of original departure. If not: passenger may self-arrange; airline reimburses reasonable costs (up to 400% of ticket price) within 14 days.
New concept
Tarmac delay rule
After 30 minutes on ground with doors closed: adequate temperature, toilet access, free water. At 2hrs: return to gate and allow disembarkation (subject to safety/security).
Expanded definition
Cancellation now includes rescheduling 1hr earlier
Rescheduling a flight 1hr+ earlier, aircraft returning to departure airport, and diversions to alternative airports now all treated as cancellations triggering full passenger rights.
Major policy change
Sequential ticketing — no-show
Passengers denied boarding on return flight because they did not take outbound = denied boarding compensation. Sequential ticketing policies no longer enforceable for point-to-point travel.
New requirement
Free personal item (40×30×15cm)
Airlines must permit one personal item free of charge. Advertised fares must include hand baggage. Low-cost carriers' ancillary models directly targeted.
Art 9 change
3-night hotel cap for extraordinary circumstances
Hotel capped at 3 nights where delay caused by extraordinary circumstances. McDonagh's unlimited obligation limited for EC cases in EU. UK261 diverges — no cap applicable in UK unless domestic amendment.
UK261 — Post-Brexit Position
Lipton v BA CityFlyer [2024] UKSC 24: The 2018 Withdrawal Act is a "Complete Code." Pre-Brexit CJEU case law is binding on UK lower courts. Post-IP completion day CJEU decisions are persuasive only — UK Supreme Court and senior appellate courts may depart. Government shelved §6 REUL Act 2023 — UK261 framework is stable.

CAA v Wizz Air
CAA enforcement action · July 2023 onwards
CAA initiated enforcement against Wizz Air UK and Wizz Air Hungary for systemic failure to pay UK261 claims. Outcome: £1.24 million refunded to passengers. Wizz Air required to reprocess all claims from 18 March 2022. Passengers with earlier claims may request reopening.

German Courts — Key Jurisdiction

Germany generates more EC261 litigation than any other EU Member State. BGH referrals to CJEU have shaped pan-European law. Airlines defending German claims face high volumes, legally sophisticated opponents, and consumer-protective courts.
