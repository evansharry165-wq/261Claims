01 · Overview
What DefendAble Is

DefendAble is a legal intelligence engine for airline legal teams. Its job is to take a disruption event — a flight delay, cancellation, diversion — and produce a structured, legally-grounded defence framework under EU Regulation 261/2004 (EC261) and its retained UK equivalent (UK261). It does not replace the lawyer. It replaces the blank page and the inconsistent first draft.

The target user is a Disruption Investigation Officer (DIO) or in-house aviation paralegal. They receive a claim, they open the engine, they enter what happened, and the system walks them through every legal question that needs answering, surfaces the relevant court judgments, identifies the weak points in the defence, and produces a brief the lawyer can use in court or in settlement negotiation.

The core design principle, stated once and never deviated from: the system creates the layout. The lawyer makes the judgment.

Regulatory Foundation
EC261/2004 — EU Regulation, applies to flights departing from any EU airport, or arriving at an EU airport on an EU-based carrier. Still applies to UK-departing flights that occurred before 31 December 2020.

UK261 — Retained UK equivalent, applies to flights departing UK airports from 1 January 2021 onwards (post-IP Completion Day). CJEU decisions are persuasive but no longer binding on UK courts following Lipton v BA CityFlyer [2024] UKSC 24.

IP Completion Day (IPCD) — 31 December 2020. The single most important date when determining which regulation applies and which court judgments are binding vs persuasive. Every analysis in the engine checks this.
02 · Legal Framework
The Legal Framework — What the Engine Applies

Everything in DefendAble flows from one foundational judgment: Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia (C-549/07), decided by the Court of Justice of the EU in 2008. This case established the two-limb test that every extraordinary circumstances defence must satisfy. If you remember one thing about the legal framework, remember this test.

The Wallentin-Hermann Two-Limb Test
Wallentin-Hermann (C-549/07) — The Defence Gateway
Limb 1
Extraordinary Circumstance
"Is the event not inherent in the normal exercise of the carrier's activity AND beyond its actual control?"
Examples that pass Limb 1
Weather · Birdstrike · Third-party ATC strike · Hidden design defect (per Matkustaja) · Government travel ban · Lawfully-called trade union strike
Examples that fail Limb 1
Technical defect · Crew illness · Own-staff wildcat strike · Carrier insolvency · Routine maintenance delay
+
Limb 2
All Reasonable Measures
"Did the carrier take all measures reasonably available to avoid the delay, exhausting human, material and financial resources — without making an unacceptable sacrifice?"
What counts as RM
Standby crew positioning · Alternative aircraft sourcing · Schedule buffers for foreseeable events · Alternate routing or CTOT bidding · Passenger re-booking and care
What is NOT required
Maintaining a back-up fleet on standby (D.S.A. v P.S.A.) · Any measure representing an unacceptable sacrifice in terms of the carrier's undertaking capacity
Both limbs must be satisfied. A defence that passes Limb 1 (extraordinary circumstance confirmed) but fails Limb 2 (reasonable measures not taken) loses. This is the most common reason a genuine extraordinary circumstance still results in a compensation award — the carrier had the event but failed to act on it.
The Sturgeon Principle — When Compensation is Owed

Under Sturgeon v Condor (C-402/07), passengers whose flight arrives 3 or more hours late at their final destination are entitled to Art 7 compensation — the same as if the flight were cancelled. The 3-hour threshold is measured against the scheduled arrival time, calculated from the moment the first aircraft door opens for disembarkation (Germanwings v Henning, C-452/13). This is not touch-down time. The engine applies this automatically.

Key Timing Rule
Arrival time = first door opens. Not wheels-on. Not gate arrival. Not seatbelt sign off. This matters in cases where the carrier argues the threshold wasn't met — a 2h 55m delay from a wheels-on perspective can become a 3h 10m delay from a door-opening perspective if the aircraft sat on the taxiway. Always check what time the doors actually opened.
Art 7 Compensation Bands

Quantum is determined by the great circle distance (straight line, point to point) between departure airport and the booked final destination — not the actual route flown. This was confirmed in Bossen v Brussels Airlines (C-559/16).

Art 7 Compensation Structure
Band 1
€250
Flights up to 1,500 km
Band 2
€400
1,500–3,500 km · or intra-EU flights over 1,500 km

03 · System Architecture
How the Engine Works — Three-Pass Architecture

The engine processes every disruption through three sequential analytical passes. Each pass feeds the next. Pass I classifies the disruption and sets the legal directive. Pass II builds an evidence-mapped causal chain and tests its integrity. Pass III delivers the legal verdict and outputs the defence brief. This sequence is deterministic — the same inputs always produce the same legal framework, every time, with no variation based on who is operating it.

The Line of Flying

One of the most important concepts in the system. The line of flying is the full rotation of an aircraft across sectors and days. If a flight is delayed, the root cause may not be on that flight at all — it may be on the aircraft's previous sector the day before. The engine detects this from language in the disruption summary (phrases like "inbound from", "prior sector", "prior rotation", "aircraft came from") and automatically prepends an "Aircraft Line of Flying" node to the causal chain before the root cause node.

This matters legally because it determines whether the extraordinary circumstance defence starts on the day of the delayed flight, or earlier. A carrier that had time overnight to source a replacement aircraft cannot claim the extraordinary circumstance from the prior day continues to justify the delay — the but-for test and causal chain integrity become critical in these cases.

05 · Technical Deep Dive
Cross-Node Intelligence — How the Chain Thinks

The most important technical build in ACT 1. Cross-node intelligence is the layer that makes the causal chain reason rather than just display. Without it, the chain is a list of events. With it, the chain understands when its own evidence structure is strong enough to defend, when it has gaps that could break the argument, and where a single weak node could collapse the entire defence.

Node Status — Four States

Every node in the causal chain is assigned a status based on the lawyer's annotations and the evidence flags they've set. The status is computed live as the lawyer works.

Strong
Annotation provided, evidence available, no high-priority weaknesses flagged by classifier. This node will hold in court.
Contested
Annotation present but high-priority weakness flagged, or evidence available but some items missing. Defensible but will face challenge.
Weak
Evidence missing, no available items confirmed. This node cannot be substantiated from current documentation. Urgent action needed.
Unworked
No annotation, no evidence status set. Not yet assessed. The lawyer has not reached this node.
Chain Integrity Signals — Three Levels

Once node statuses are computed, the chain's overall integrity is assessed by propagating signals upward from each node. The chain signal determines the colour of the Chain Integrity meter in Pass III and influences the defence brief's opening assessment.

Solid
All worked nodes are Strong or Contested. No critical gaps. The causal chain as documented is defensible. Chain Integrity meter shows green.
Fragile
At least one Weak node exists, or multiple Contested nodes. The chain has identifiable gaps that opposing counsel will target. Amber meter. Lawyer action needed before this can be filed.
Broken
A Weak node exists at a critical position (typically root cause or direct propagation link). The argument cannot be sustained as currently documented. Red meter. Settlement should be considered.
The But-For Statement

The but-for statement is the engine's plain-language summary of the causal chain's legal argument. It appears as a banner above Pass II and updates live. Its function is to let the lawyer read the argument as a single sentence before they've worked every node — and to make clear what the defence will have to prove.

Example: "But for the Eurocontrol ATFM restriction (French FIR) which caused a 3h 20m slot delay and exhausted crew FDP, flight EZY8812 GVA–MAN would have arrived within the scheduled tolerance."

If the causal chain breaks — if a voluntary carrier decision intervenes between the extraordinary circumstance and the delay — the but-for statement should reflect that break. This is the NI/HZ v European Air Charter principle: a carrier decision to wait for delayed passengers can sever the direct causal link, breaking the Art 5(3) defence even where the original extraordinary circumstance was genuine.

07 · Terminology
Glossary — Key Terms to Know
Extraordinary Circumstance
An event not inherent in the normal exercise of airline activity and beyond the carrier's actual control. Must satisfy Wallentin-Hermann Limb 1. If absent, there is no Art 5(3) defence regardless of what the carrier did.
Reasonable Measures (RM)
All steps reasonably available to avoid the delay — Wallentin-Hermann Limb 2. Exhausting human, material and financial resources. The most commonly failed part of the defence even where extraordinary circumstances are clearly established.
Causal Chain
The sequence of events from the root cause through to the passenger's delay at final destination. Every link in the chain must be established for the Art 5(3) defence to hold. A broken link means the extraordinary circumstance cannot be said to have caused the delay.
But-For Statement
The plain-language articulation of the causal chain: "but for [extraordinary event], the flight would not have been delayed." The engine derives this automatically from the confirmed chain nodes.
Line of Flying
An aircraft's full rotation across sectors and days. A delay on flight B may be caused by an event that happened to the same aircraft on flight A the previous day. Establishing the line of flying is critical to tracing root cause across rotations.
ATFM / CTOT
Air Traffic Flow Management / Calculated Take-Off Time. Eurocontrol assigns CTOTs to aircraft when network capacity is restricted. A CTOT is evidence of a third-party restriction. CFMU documentation is the key evidence item for ATFM defences.
FDP
Flight Duty Period. The maximum period a crew can be on duty under EASA Part-ORO regulations. If FDP is reached, the crew cannot legally operate. An FDP breach causes a cancellation. Critically: if the FDP breach was foreseeable hours in advance, the question is always whether standby crew could have been positioned as RM.
MEL
Minimum Equipment List. Allows an aircraft to operate with certain items unserviceable, under defined conditions and time limits (Category A/B/C/D). A MEL Category C dispatch (10-day limit) means the fault was known — it is a foreseeable circumstance, not extraordinary. This weakens any subsequent technical delay claim.
IPCD
IP Completion Day — 31 December 2020. Flights departing UK airports before this date fall under EC261. Flights departing UK airports from 1 January 2021 fall under UK261. CJEU decisions are binding for EC261 but only persuasive for UK261 cases.
DIO
Disruption Investigation Officer. The primary user of DefendAble within an airline legal or operations team. Responsible for compiling the disruption record and defence file. DefendAble is built around the DIO's workflow.
ICC Log
Integrated Control Centre log. The real-time operational record kept by airline Network Control during a disruption. The primary source document for Pass I input. DefendAble's ICC text mode is designed to accept this format directly.
Chain Integrity Meter
The visual indicator in Pass III showing whether the causal chain as annotated is Solid (green / all nodes strong), Fragile (amber / gaps present), or Broken (red / critical node weak). The colour of the meter is derived from the cross-node intelligence signals, not a manual assessment.
Art 9 Obligations
Care obligations that survive extraordinary circumstances. Meals, refreshments, hotel accommodation, communication. These are NOT disapplied by Art 5(3) — an airline can win on extraordinary circumstances and still be sanctioned separately for failing to provide Art 9 care (McDonagh v Ryanair).
Art 14 Obligation
Written notification of passenger rights at check-in. An independent obligation — a carrier can satisfy Art 5(3) and still be sanctioned by a national enforcement body for failing to post the Art 14 notice (Bacău Air Centre). A frequent oversight in high-volume operations.

09 · Product Strategy
What DefendAble Is — and What It Is Not

This is the section to read before any demo or meeting. Getting this framing right matters.

What DefendAble Is
A legal reasoning engine that applies EC261/UK261 logic to structured disruption data and produces a defence framework for lawyer review. Its IP is the workflow: the classification, the causal chain structure, the cross-node intelligence, the caselaw application. It is the layer that sits between raw operational data and the lawyer's desk.
What DefendAble Is Not
A data system. It does not hold flight data, operational records, or claim files. Those exist in the airline's own operational infrastructure. DefendAble is a downstream reasoning layer that processes whatever structured data is fed to it. The data integration is an implementation project, not a product feature.
The Data Integration Story
Airlines already hold the data DefendAble needs: disruption reason, timing data, crew records, ATFM documentation, METAR/TAF. This sits in operational data systems (data warehouses, lake houses, case management platforms). The integration question is: what does a structured disruption record look like coming out of those systems, and how closely does it map to DefendAble's input format? If that mapping is close, the integration is a data engineering project. If it's not close, it's a data transformation layer. DefendAble does not need the airline to change its infrastructure — it sits downstream of what they already have.
The Demo's Job

In a demo to airline legal teams or DIOs, the scenarios in the engine should be introduced with the framing: "This is a disruption record as it would arrive from your operational data layer." That framing makes the integration story concrete without requiring any live system connection. The audience will immediately map it to their own data — they know what their ICC logs look like.

The demo has three moments that always land: (1) the system correctly classifying a complex multi-node disruption and identifying the legal weakness before the lawyer does; (2) the system catching a loss early — a case where the carrier has no extraordinary circumstances defence — and recommending settle clearly and with cited authority; (3) the caselaw modal opening to a directly linkable EUR-Lex judgment, demonstrating that the legal foundations are independently verifiable.

10 · Pass II Redesign
The LOF Verification Workflow — Pass II Rebuilt

The original Pass II displayed the causal chain derived from ICC text and asked the lawyer to annotate each node. The problem: fact verification and legal analysis happened simultaneously, from the same narrative. The lawyer never had a dedicated moment to confirm the factual picture before legal flags fired. This was identified as a structural gap — the LOF and legal workflow lacked the lawyer-in-the-loop relationship they needed.

The redesigned Pass II separates the workflow into two explicit stages:

Stage 1 — Factual Verification. The Line of Flying is presented as an interactive evidence-backed timeline. Each node represents a sector, event, or recovery action in the aircraft's full rotation — not just the claimed flight. The lawyer verifies each node against actual evidence: CFMU slot confirmations, METAR records, crew FDP calculations, ops logs. Evidence documents open in a slide-in viewer alongside the LOF timeline so the lawyer can verify facts against the source simultaneously. Missing evidence can be logged as a request, which is tracked. When the lawyer is satisfied, they confirm the node.

Stage 2 — Legal Analysis. Only after the factual record is confirmed does the legal analysis fire — and it fires against the confirmed facts, not the original ICC narrative. This is the critical difference. A legal flag about an extraordinary circumstance is only meaningful if the underlying fact has been verified against contemporaneous evidence. The two stages enforce this discipline.

Core Design Principle
The system creates the layout. The lawyer makes the judgement.

DefendAble's role is to surface the right facts, evidence, and legal context at the right moment — not to make the defence decision. The lawyer confirms, challenges, or requests more evidence at each node. The engine reacts to what is confirmed, not to what the ICC text says.

Interactive command bar. Within the LOF interface, the lawyer can type natural language requests alongside the timeline — for example: "pull line of flying for G-EZFM on 15JUN24" or "get Eurocontrol CFMU docs for GVA-PMI". The system responds by pulling and updating — either adding a new LOF node (e.g. prior day rotation), or adding evidence documents to an existing node. The lawyer reviews the update and confirms or challenges it.

Evidence viewer. Each evidence item opens a structured document card in a slide-in panel — not a raw external page, but a parsed, key-field-highlighted view of the document. A METAR card shows the wind, decoded, with the critical timestamp and speed highlighted against the certified aircraft limit. A CFMU card shows the slot reference, delay, and regulation applied. For public source documents (METAR archive, Eurocontrol NOP), a View Source ↗ link takes the lawyer to the actual document for independent verification. This is how the lawyer verifies the LOF against the evidence it is based on, within the same interface.

Confirm Line of Flying. When the factual record is confirmed across all nodes, the lawyer triggers "Confirm Line of Flying" — which locks Stage 1 and passes the verified record to legal analysis. The LOF confirmation screen shows the full chain summary: confirmed nodes, outstanding evidence, causal chain narrative, delay quantum. This is the handover point from fact to law.

11 · Strategic Review
Ed's Email — The Strategic Questions and Where They Lead

A strategic review of DefendAble identified four questions that need answers before the product can be sold to an airline:

1. Who is the buyer? The most likely buyer is not the DIO or paralegal (the user) — it is the Head of Legal or General Counsel at the airline, who signs off on operational tooling. The product needs to speak their language at the demo: cost per case, portfolio exposure visibility, time to defence, consistency of legal output across the team. The demo should land for someone who manages a legal budget, not just for someone who files defences.

2. The 80/20 problem. DefendAble has built the right 20%: the legal intelligence layer. The hard 80% is data integration — getting structured disruption records out of the airline's operational infrastructure (data warehouse, ICC system, operational control) and into DefendAble's input layer reliably and at scale. This is not a legal problem. It is a data engineering problem. The answer: the DIO enters the data manually in Phase 1. The integration comes later, airline by airline, as part of the commercial agreement. The product is still valuable without the integration — but the integration is what makes it transformational.

3. Liability positioning. DefendAble provides legal intelligence, not legal advice. The lawyer makes the judgement. The system surfaces the facts, the evidence structure, the applicable authority, and the causal chain analysis. The decision is always the lawyer's. This is not just a disclaimer — it is the design principle. The two-stage LOF workflow (verify facts first, then fire legal analysis) enforces this operationally. The lawyer cannot skip fact verification. This is the "human in the loop" architecture.

4. Law keeps moving. The legal landscape changes: new CJEU rulings, domestic court decisions, CAA enforcement positions. DefendAble's CASELAW_DB and reference repository are designed to be updated as the law evolves. New legal references added to the reference document should always be cross-checked against the engine's CASELAW_DB to ensure the analysis layer reflects current law. The reference document is the knowledge foundation; the engine applies it.

Two-Product Vision (Emerging)
DefendAble Analyse — The legal intelligence engine built in Phase 3. Takes a disruption record, produces a three-pass legal analysis, LOF verification workflow, and defence brief. Used per-case by the DIO or paralegal.

DefendAble Manage — The case management and portfolio layer planned for ACT 2. Where cases live after analysis: portfolio tracking, exposure calculation across batches, workflow states (claim received → under investigation → defence prepared → resolved), and the connection to the airline's data infrastructure.
12 · What's Next
ACT 2 — The Case Management Layer

ACT 1 built the intelligence engine: the thing that takes a disruption and produces a legal framework. ACT 2 is the layer that sits above and around it: where cases live once they've been analysed, how portfolios of claims are tracked across a carrier's operation, how the engine connects to the airline's data infrastructure, and how the DIO manages work in progress across multiple open cases simultaneously.

ACT 2 is a planning session before it is a build. The LOF workflow redesign (Section 10) is the final element of ACT 1 — it completes the legal intelligence layer. ACT 2 builds the operational layer around it. The questions it needs to answer: what does a case lifecycle look like from receipt of claim to settlement or court? How are cases grouped by disruption event? What does a DIO's daily dashboard look like? And critically — what does the data connection between an airline's operational infrastructure and DefendAble's input layer look like in practice?

ACT 2 Key Questions to Plan
Case portfolio view: How many live claims, what stage, what's the total exposure?

Event-based batching: One disruption event typically generates multiple claims from the same flight. ACT 2 needs to group these and allow one defence to serve the batch.

Data connection: What is the input format from a data warehouse perspective? What fields are mandatory vs enrichable by the DIO? The Databricks loop — how does the airline's operational record become DefendAble's input?

Workflow states: New claim → Under investigation → Defence prepared → Settled / Filed / Defended. The engine needs to know where in this lifecycle a case sits.

The Jet2 opportunity: The first target commercial relationship. The demo needs to land for a Head of Legal who manages a team of DIOs across a substantial UK-domestic and short-haul European operation. The portfolio view — how many live claims, what's the exposure, where is each one — is what they will ask for first.
