I. Extraordinary Circumstances — The Two-Limb Test
Foundation
C-549/07 · CJEU · 22 December 2008
Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
A technical fault in an aircraft cannot constitute an extraordinary circumstance unless it stems from events not inherent in the normal exercise of airline activity and beyond the carrier's actual control. A malfunction discovered during pre-flight checks or routine maintenance does not qualify merely because it is unexpected.
Key Propositions
  • A technical defect is not extraordinary merely because it is statistically rare.
  • Events inherent in the normal exercise of airline activity — including unexpected technical failures — are not extraordinary circumstances.
  • The airline must take all measures reasonably available, exhausting human, material and financial resources, even where the situation was not caused by the airline.
Key Passage
“A technical problem in an aircraft cannot be covered by the concept of 'extraordinary circumstances' unless it stems from events which, by their nature or origin, are not inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier concerned and are beyond its actual control.”
Key Paragraphs
Para. 23 — the foundational two-limb test: extraordinary circumstances must not be inherent in the normal exercise of airline activity, and must be beyond the carrier's actual control

C-344/04 · CJEU · 10 January 2006
IATA and ELFAA v Department for Transport
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Regulation EC No 261/2004 is valid. The standardised and immediate assistance measures it establishes for passengers in the event of denied boarding, cancellation or long delay are proportionate and do not conflict with the Montreal Convention, which governs individual claims for compensatory damages on different legal bases.
Key Passage
“The measures introduced by Regulation No 261/2004 are standardised and immediate, distinct from compensation of an individual nature for which a request can be made pursuant to the Montreal Convention.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 36–49 — EC261 validity upheld; standardised compensation regime is proportionate and operates on a distinct legal basis from the individual damage remedy under the Montreal Convention

II. Technical Defects
C-257/14 · CJEU · 17 September 2015
Van der Lans v KLM
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Technical problems caused by premature failure of aircraft components are inherent in the normal exercise of airline operations and do not constitute extraordinary circumstances under Art 5(3), even where the failure was unexpected and not attributable to inadequate maintenance. Airlines must be taken to assume the normal operational risks of their activity.
Key Propositions
  • Unexpected technical failures are not extraordinary circumstances simply because they were not anticipated or are statistically unusual.
  • Technical defects are inherent in the operational risks airlines accept as part of normal activity, regardless of whether maintenance was adequate.
  • The distinction from extraordinary circumstances is the nature and origin of the event, not the airline's subjective expectation.
Key Passage
“Such technical problems are inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier. Since the replacement of a defective component is part of normal maintenance of an aircraft, such a technical problem cannot constitute an 'extraordinary circumstance'.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 36–42 — premature failure of aircraft components is inherent in the normal operational risks of airline activity, even where failure was unexpected and maintenance was adequate

[2014] EWCA Civ 791 · 27 June 2014
Jet2.com Ltd v Huzar
England and Wales Court of Appeal (Civil Division)
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
UK Court of Appeal confirmed, following Wallentin-Hermann, that a technical defect in an aircraft is not an extraordinary circumstance under EC261. A fuel pipe defect causing significant delay did not qualify. UK courts apply the same test as the CJEU: the defect must not be inherent in normal airline operations.
Key Passage
“A technical problem which is inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier, such as an unexpected fault occurring, is not an extraordinary circumstance within the meaning of Article 5(3) of the Regulation.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 22–40 — UK Court of Appeal applies Wallentin-Hermann without modification: technical defects are not extraordinary circumstances regardless of their unexpectedness or the carrier's maintenance record

C-385/23 · CJEU · 13 June 2024
Matkustaja A v Finnair Oyj
Court of Justice of the European Union
Supports Defence
Ratio
A latent design defect in a new aircraft model, where the defect was the first worldwide occurrence on an aircraft in service for only five months, constitutes extraordinary circumstances where the aircraft manufacturer confirms the failure resulted from a hidden design defect affecting all aircraft of the same type, the failure impinges on flight safety, and the carrier had no power to identify or remedy the defect. The timing of the manufacturer's recognition of the defect is irrelevant.
Key Propositions — Four-Part Test
  • Limb 1: The aircraft manufacturer confirms the failure resulted from a hidden design defect.
  • Limb 2: The failure was the first worldwide occurrence on aircraft of that type.
  • Limb 3: The defect affects all aircraft of the same type (not isolated to one airframe).
  • Limb 4: The defect impinges on flight safety and the carrier had no power to identify or remedy it.
Key Passage
“The carrier has no power to identify and remedy a latent defect in design; it cannot be considered to exercise control in this scenario. The classification of a hidden design defect as extraordinary is not subject to the condition that the manufacturer must have revealed its existence before the failure occurred.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 23–47 — four-part test for a latent design defect to constitute extraordinary circumstances; it is irrelevant that the manufacturer only recognised and confirmed the defect after the cancellation occurred

C-411/23 · CJEU · 13 June 2024
D. S.A. v P. S.A.
Court of Justice of the European Union
Supports Defence
Ratio
The extraordinary circumstances defence for a hidden design defect is available even where the aircraft manufacturer had informed the carrier of the defect's existence several months before the flight concerned, provided the carrier still had no power to remedy the defect at the time of the disruption. Carriers are also NOT required to maintain a back-up fleet of aircraft and crew on standby.
Key Propositions
  • Prior knowledge of a hidden design defect does not disqualify the extraordinary circumstances defence if the carrier still could not remedy the defect.
  • The test is whether the carrier had the power to identify AND remedy the defect before the disruption — awareness alone does not establish control.
  • Carriers are not required to maintain a back-up fleet on standby as part of reasonable measures under Art 5(3) — this would be an unacceptable sacrifice.
Key Passage
“Article 5(3) does not require air carriers, in a general and indiscriminate manner, to adopt a given preventive measure, such as having a back-up fleet of aircraft and the corresponding crew on standby.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 25–45 — prior knowledge of a hidden design defect does not defeat the Art 5(3) defence if the carrier still had no power to remedy it; carriers are not required to maintain a back-up fleet as a general reasonable measure

III. Own-Staff Industrial Action and Crew Issues
C-195/17 · CJEU · 17 April 2018
Krüsemann and Others v TUIfly
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
A spontaneous strike (wildcat strike) by an airline's own cabin crew in reaction to restructuring plans does not constitute an extraordinary circumstance within Art 5(3) EC261. Labour disputes and industrial action by the carrier's own employees are inherent in the normal management of the airline undertaking.
Key Passage
“A 'wildcat strike' by cabin crew of an air carrier, which spontaneously began, does not fall within the concept of 'extraordinary circumstances' since it is part of the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier concerned.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 34–46 — own-staff wildcat strike is part of the normal exercise of airline activity; labour disputes involving the carrier's own employees do not constitute extraordinary circumstances

[2024] UKSC 24 · 10 July 2024
Lipton and another v BA CityFlyer Ltd
UK Supreme Court — First ruling on extraordinary circumstances
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
The UK Supreme Court unanimously held that a pilot unexpectedly falling ill does not constitute an extraordinary circumstance under EC261. Crew illness is inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of an air carrier, just as the wear of mechanical components is inherent. It is irrelevant that the pilot fell ill while off duty: a pilot's attendance or non-attendance for work is an inherent part of the carrier's operating system.
Key Propositions
  • Pilot and crew illness is NEVER an extraordinary circumstance — it is inherent in the normal exercise of airline activity.
  • It makes no difference whether the crew member fell ill on duty or off duty, or from an unusual illness.
  • The purpose of EC261 is standardised, immediate compensation: excessive granular fact-finding as to the cause of illness is inconsistent with this purpose.
  • Pre-IP Completion Day (31 Dec 2020) flights departing EU airports are governed by EC261, not UK261.
Key Passage
“A pilot, because of his role, is 'as much part of the operating system' as the mechanical components of the aircraft. Their attendance or non-attendance for work is an inherent part of the carrier's operating system.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 42–66 — crew illness is inherent in the normal exercise of airline operations regardless of whether it occurred on or off duty; the purpose of EC261 is standardised, immediate compensation inconsistent with granular fact-finding as to the cause of illness

IV. Weather, ATFM, and Causal Chain Integrity
C-294/10 · CJEU · 12 May 2011
Eglitis and Mäkinen v Latvijas Republikas Ekonomikas ministrija
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
An air carrier must take all measures available, including planning adequate time buffers into scheduling, to avoid delays or cancellations caused by foreseeable extraordinary circumstances. The carrier does not have to make intolerable sacrifices in terms of capacity but must deploy all available resources at the time of the disruption.
Key Passage
“The air carrier is required to take all measures available — including planning adequate time buffers — when faced with an extraordinary circumstance, unless such measures represent an unacceptable sacrifice in terms of the capacity of its undertaking.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 25–30 — all reasonable measures test: the carrier must use all human, material and financial resources available, including planning adequate time buffers for foreseeable extraordinary circumstances
Open full judgment on EUR-Lex ↗ CELEX: 62010CJ0294
T-656/24 · EU General Court · 4 March 2026
NI, HZ v European Air Charter AG
General Court of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Where an extraordinary circumstance affects an earlier flight rotation, a carrier may only rely on that circumstance for a subsequent delayed flight if there is a sufficiently direct causal link between the extraordinary event and the later delay. A voluntary operational decision by the carrier — such as choosing to wait for delayed passengers from an earlier rotation — can break that causal link if the decision, and not the original extraordinary event, becomes the determining cause of the later delay.
Key Propositions
  • An extraordinary circumstance on one rotation can only support an Art 5(3) defence for a later rotation if a sufficiently direct causal link exists between the two.
  • A voluntary carrier decision (e.g. waiting for delayed passengers) can break the causal chain if that decision becomes the determining cause of the subsequent flight's delay.
  • The causal chain analysis is strict: the but-for test is insufficient — the extraordinary event must be the determining, proximate cause of the delay.
  • Airlines must assess whether their operational response choices will break the causal chain — even reasonable, passenger-friendly choices can do so.
Key Passage
“A decision taken by the air carrier to wait for passengers of the first flight arose between the extraordinary circumstance and the delay of the flight at issue. That decision is capable of breaking the direct causal link if it constitutes the determining cause for that delay and the carrier was not obliged to take it.”
Key Paragraphs
Key ratio — a voluntary operational decision by the carrier (such as waiting for delayed passengers from an earlier rotation) can break the direct causal link between an extraordinary circumstance and a subsequent flight's delay if that decision becomes the determining cause of the later delay

VI. Compensation Entitlement — Sturgeon Principle
C-402/07 and C-432/07 · CJEU · 19 November 2009
Sturgeon v Condor Flugdienst and Böck v Air France
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Passengers whose flight is delayed by three or more hours at their final destination are in a situation comparable to that of passengers whose flight is cancelled, and are entitled to compensation under Art 7 EC261. The right to compensation arises at the point of arrival being three or more hours late, not merely at the time of departure delay.
Key Passage
“Passengers whose flights are delayed may rely on the right to compensation laid down in Article 7 of the Regulation when they suffer, because of a delay, a loss of time equal to or in excess of three hours.”
Key Paragraphs
Para. 61 — passengers delayed 3 or more hours at their final destination are in a situation comparable to passengers whose flights are cancelled and are entitled to Art 7 compensation

C-581/10 and C-629/10 · CJEU · 23 October 2012
Nelson v Deutsche Lufthansa and TUI Travel and Others
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Confirms the Sturgeon principle: passengers experiencing delays of 3 or more hours at their final destination are entitled to Art 7 compensation. The right to standardised compensation for long delay is valid and proportionate, and does not require the delay to have occurred at the last minute.
Key Passage
“The standardised and immediate nature of the right to compensation under Regulation 261/2004 makes it possible for passengers to rely on the right in the event of a delay of three hours or more.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 33–40 — Sturgeon principle affirmed; standardised and immediate compensation for delay is compatible with the Montreal Convention, which operates on a distinct legal basis

C-11/11 · CJEU · 26 February 2013
Folkerts v Air France
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Where a passenger on a connecting flight journey arrives at their final destination 3 or more hours late, compensation is due under Art 7 EC261 regardless of whether any individual flight leg was itself delayed by that margin. The 3-hour threshold is assessed at the final destination, not at each intermediate stop.
Key Passage
“The compensation owed is calculated by reference to the scheduled arrival time of the final flight, not by reference to the arrival time of any connecting flight on which the passenger was carried to a stopping point.”
Key Paragraphs
Para. 32 — the 3-hour compensation threshold is assessed against scheduled vs actual arrival time at the final destination specified in the passenger's booking, not at intermediate stops

VII. Art 9 Care Obligations and Art 12 Further Compensation
C-12/11 · CJEU · 31 January 2013
McDonagh v Ryanair
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Even where a flight is cancelled due to extraordinary circumstances such as volcanic ash cloud, the carrier remains obliged to provide care and assistance under Art 9 EC261 (refreshments, meals, accommodation and communication). Extraordinary circumstances do not suspend the Art 9 care obligation; it only ceases if the passenger has been offered a full refund under Art 8.
Key Propositions
  • Art 9 care obligations (meals, accommodation, communication) survive even during extraordinary circumstances — they are not disapplied by Art 5(3).
  • Art 5(3) only exempts airlines from Art 7 compensation, not from Art 9 care or Art 8 re-routing obligations.
Key Passage
“The obligation to provide care for stranded passengers under Article 9 applies even in cases of 'extraordinary circumstances' — the existence of such circumstances does not extinguish that obligation.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 29–44 — Art 9 care obligations (meals, accommodation, communication) apply even in extraordinary circumstances; Art 5(3) only exempts from Art 7 monetary compensation

VIII. Jurisdiction, Scope and Limitation
[2014] EWCA Civ 845 · 11 July 2014
Dawson v Thomson Airways Ltd
England and Wales Court of Appeal (Civil Division)
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
EC261 claims in England and Wales are subject to the 6-year limitation period under s.2 Limitation Act 1980, not the 2-year period under Article 35 of the Montreal Convention. EC261 creates a separate statutory right of action, and the Montreal limitation period does not apply to it.
Key Passage
“The claim under the Regulation is not a claim pursuant to the Montreal Convention; it is a claim under EC Regulation 261/2004. The applicable limitation period is six years under the Limitation Act 1980.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 23–35 — EC261 claims in England and Wales are subject to the 6-year limitation period under s.2 Limitation Act 1980, not the 2-year Montreal Convention period; the two are distinct remedies

IX. Third-Party Industrial Action and ATC Disruption
C-28/20 · CJEU · 23 March 2021
Airhelp Ltd v Swiss International Air Lines AG
Court of Justice of the European Union
Supports Defence
Ratio
A planned, officially announced strike by an airline's own cabin crew — as distinct from a spontaneous wildcat strike — constitutes an extraordinary circumstance within Art 5(3) EC261 where: (i) the strike was called by an independent trade union acting in accordance with applicable labour law; and (ii) the strike falls outside the carrier's control because it was organised in conformity with the legal framework governing labour relations. The key distinction from Krüsemann (wildcat strike) is whether the industrial action was conducted through lawful, structured trade union processes.
Key Propositions
  • An officially called trade union strike by own staff CAN be extraordinary — the Krüsemann rule (own-staff IA is never extraordinary) applies only to wildcat/spontaneous action, not lawful structured strike action.
  • The test is: (a) was the strike called by a recognised, independent trade union; and (b) was it conducted in conformity with the applicable legal framework (notice requirements, negotiation procedures, etc.)?
  • Where both conditions are met, the carrier lacks actual control over the event and the first Wallentin-Hermann limb may be satisfied.
  • Limb 2 (all reasonable measures) still applies: the carrier must demonstrate it took all steps available to mitigate the disruption.
Key Passage
“A strike called by a trade union following a call to industrial action is not an event inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier where the carrier does not have actual control over the action taken by an independent representative body acting in conformity with the law.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 27–43 — planned, lawfully-called own-staff trade union strike is extraordinary (distinguishing Krüsemann C-195/17 which applied to wildcat action); both Wallentin-Hermann limbs remain applicable

X. Pandemic and Government-Imposed Travel Restrictions
C-68/22 · CJEU · 7 July 2022
Wizz Air Hungary Ltd v TB
Court of Justice of the European Union
Supports Defence
Ratio
A government-imposed entry ban or travel restriction constitutes an extraordinary circumstance for the purposes of Art 5(3) EC261 where it prevents a flight from operating or renders its operation commercially impossible, provided the carrier could not have avoided the circumstance by taking all reasonable measures. The regulation's framework is not suspended during a pandemic but individual cancellations caused directly by government restrictions may benefit from the Art 5(3) defence.
Key Propositions
  • A government entry ban or travel prohibition targeting a specific route or territory is not inherent in normal airline operations and is beyond the carrier's actual control.
  • The carrier must still demonstrate all reasonable measures were taken — including offering alternative routes, re-booking, or seeking regulatory clarity before cancelling.
  • EC261 is not disapplied during a public health emergency; each cancellation must be assessed individually against the Art 5(3) test.
  • A general anticipatory cancellation of flights before any binding governmental restriction is in force does not benefit from Art 5(3) even if restrictions later materialise.
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 22–38 — government-imposed travel ban = extraordinary circumstance if it directly caused the cancellation and the carrier could not avoid it; EC261 is not suspended during a pandemic; pre-emptive cancellation before any binding restriction cannot benefit from Art 5(3)

XI. Connecting Flights and Operating Carrier Liability
C-163/18 · CJEU · 11 July 2019
Harms and Others v Vueling Airlines SA
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Where a passenger holds a single booking for a connecting itinerary involving two separate flights operated by two different carriers, and the first flight (operated by carrier A) arrives late causing the passenger to miss their connection on the second flight (operated by carrier B), carrier A is the "operating carrier" for the purposes of Art 5(3) EC261 only in respect of the flight it operated. However, if the ticket was issued as a single booking, the carrier that caused the missed connection by its delay bears the compensation liability for the resulting total journey delay.
Key Propositions
  • On a single-booking connecting itinerary, responsibility for delay at the final destination tracks the carrier whose delay caused the passenger to miss the connection.
  • The Montreal Convention's limitation of liability does not limit or extinguish EC261 compensation claims against the operating carrier responsible for the delay.
  • A package travel operator that issues a ticket combining two separate carriers' flights does not thereby become the operating carrier for EC261 purposes.
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 28–45 — operating carrier liability on connecting flights; the carrier whose delay caused the missed connection bears responsibility for total journey delay at the final destination on a single-booking itinerary

XII. Carrier Insolvency and Financial Circumstances
C-532/17 · CJEU · 4 October 2018
Bundesverband der Verbraucherzentralen v Niki Luftfahrt GmbH
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Financial insolvency and the consequent cessation of operations do not constitute extraordinary circumstances within Art 5(3) EC261. A carrier's financial difficulties — including entry into insolvency proceedings — are inherent in the normal conduct of a commercial airline undertaking. Passengers whose flights are cancelled as a result of an airline becoming insolvent retain their Art 7 compensation entitlement, which must be pursued as a claim in the insolvency estate.
Key Propositions
  • Financial insolvency is not an extraordinary circumstance under Art 5(3) — it is an inherent commercial risk of operating as an airline.
  • Passengers retain Art 7 compensation rights and must submit as creditors in the insolvency proceedings.
  • The carrier's inability to pay due to insolvency does not extinguish the liability; it merely affects enforceability.
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 20–32 — carrier financial insolvency is not extraordinary circumstances; it is inherent in normal commercial airline operations; passenger Art 7 compensation rights survive and are pursued as insolvency claims

XIII. Re-Routing, Denied Boarding and Downgrading
C-74/19 · CJEU · 26 March 2020
LE v Austrian Airlines AG
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Where a passenger accepts re-routing offered by the carrier under Art 8(1)(b) EC261 and arrives at the final destination on an alternative flight, the 3-hour compensation threshold under the Sturgeon principle is calculated by reference to the scheduled arrival time of the original cancelled flight — not the arrival time of the rerouted flight. A carrier cannot reduce or extinguish the compensation payable by re-routing a passenger onto a flight that arrives more than 3 hours after the original scheduled arrival.
Key Propositions
  • The reference point for calculating delay and compensation is the original scheduled arrival time, not the arrival time of any replacement flight.
  • Acceptance of re-routing does not waive or reduce compensation rights under Art 7 where the rerouted arrival is 3 or more hours late against the original schedule.
  • Carriers cannot use Art 8 re-routing to extinguish Art 7 compensation obligations.
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 36–50 — delay and compensation threshold calculated against the original scheduled arrival time; re-routing under Art 8 does not reduce or eliminate compensation owed under Art 7 where the re-routed arrival is 3+ hours after original schedule

XIV. Art 14 Notification Obligations and Information Rights
C-355/18 to C-357/18 and C-479/18 · CJEU · 22 April 2021
Bacău Air Centre SA and Others v ANPC (Joined Cases)
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Art 14 EC261 imposes an obligation on the operating carrier to inform passengers of their rights at check-in (by way of a notice at the check-in desk) and upon denial of boarding, cancellation or long delay. Failure to provide this notice constitutes a breach of the regulation independent of whether Art 7 compensation or Art 5(3) defences are in issue. National enforcement authorities may sanction carriers for breach of Art 14 even where the disruption was caused by extraordinary circumstances that would have otherwise provided a complete defence to Art 7 compensation.
Key Propositions
  • Art 14 notice obligations are independent of the extraordinary circumstances defence — failure to notify is a separate breach even where Art 5(3) would defeat any Art 7 claim.
  • The Art 14 notice must be in writing in the prescribed form; verbal notification alone is insufficient.
  • National enforcement bodies may sanction airlines for Art 14 breaches under their domestic implementing legislation.
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 44–58 — Art 14 notification obligations are independent of the Art 5(3) extraordinary circumstances defence; carriers must provide written notice of rights on each occasion of disruption regardless of whether compensation is ultimately payable

XV. Arrival Time and Distance — Defining the Compensation Threshold
C-452/13 · CJEU · 4 September 2014
Germanwings GmbH v Henning
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
The moment at which a flight's actual arrival time must be assessed for the purposes of the three-hour Sturgeon threshold is the time at which at least one of the aircraft doors is opened, on the assumption that at that moment passengers are permitted to leave the aircraft. This is the "actual arrival time" for EC261 purposes — not the time the aircraft touches down, not the time it reaches the gate, and not the time the seatbelt sign is switched off.
Key Propositions
  • Arrival time = moment at least one aircraft door is opened for disembarkation — not touch-down time.
  • Carriers cannot argue that the 3-hour threshold was not met by reference to wheels-on time where the door-opening time was 3 or more hours after the scheduled arrival.
  • This definition applies consistently for Art 7 compensation and for the cancellation re-routing obligations under Art 5(1)(c)(iii).
Key Passage
“The actual arrival time of a flight corresponds to the time at which at least one of the doors of the aircraft is opened, the assumption being that, at that moment, the passengers are permitted to leave the aircraft.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 18–26 — actual arrival time = first door opens for disembarkation; not wheels-on, not gate arrival; this is the moment against which the 3-hour Sturgeon threshold is assessed
Open full judgment on EUR-Lex ↗ CELEX: 62013CJ0452
C-559/16 · CJEU · 7 September 2017
Bossen and Others v Brussels Airlines SA/NV
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
The distance to be used for determining the applicable Art 7 compensation band is the great circle distance (orthodromic distance) between the departure airport and the final destination airport specified in the passenger's booking, calculated in a straight line between those two points regardless of the actual flight path. This means the quantum bands (€250/€400/€600 at 1,500km / 1,500–3,500km / 3,500km+) are calculated by reference to the booked endpoint, not intermediate stops on connecting flights.
Key Propositions
  • The Art 7 compensation band is determined by the great circle (orthodromic) distance between the departure airport and the final booked destination.
  • The actual flight route flown — including any diversions or connecting legs — is irrelevant to the distance calculation.
  • On connecting itineraries under a single booking, the final destination determines the band, not the point of missed connection.
  • Where the flight actually flown is a direct route shorter than the booked connecting journey, the booked endpoint still governs.
Key Passage
“The distance of the flight must be determined on the basis of the great circle route method, between the airport of departure and the airport of final destination.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 24–34 — Art 7 quantum bands determined by great circle distance between departure and booked final destination; actual flight path and connecting legs are irrelevant to the calculation

XVI. Art 5(1)(c) — Cancellation Notification and the 14-Day Rule
C-83/10 · CJEU · 13 October 2011
Sousa Rodriguez and Others v Air France SA
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
Where a flight is cancelled after departure — whether through diversion, return to the departure airport, or early landing — and passengers do not reach their final destination, that event constitutes a cancellation under Art 5 EC261, not merely a delay. In such circumstances the carrier's obligations under Art 8 (reimbursement and re-routing) and Art 9 (care) are triggered, as are, where applicable, the Art 7 compensation provisions. Passengers are entitled to the choice between reimbursement and re-routing, not merely to eventual re-routing decided unilaterally by the carrier.
Key Propositions
  • A flight that returns to its departure airport after take-off or makes an early landing short of the final destination constitutes a cancellation, not a delay, for EC261 purposes.
  • Passengers in this situation are entitled to the full Art 8 choice: reimbursement of the ticket price, or re-routing at the earliest opportunity, or re-routing at a later date of their choosing.
  • The carrier cannot unilaterally impose re-routing and deny the passenger their right to reimbursement.
  • Art 7 compensation may also be payable in such circumstances, subject to Art 5(3) extraordinary circumstances defence.
Key Passage
“Where a flight turns back to its departure airport after take-off or makes an early landing short of the final destination, that event constitutes a cancellation within the meaning of Article 2(l) of the Regulation.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 29–44 — a return to departure airport or early landing constitutes a cancellation, triggering Art 7/8/9 obligations; passengers are entitled to the full Art 8 choice including reimbursement, not merely carrier-imposed re-routing

XVII. Denied Boarding — Scope and Definition
C-22/11 · CJEU · 4 October 2012
Finnair Oyj v Timy Lassooy
Court of Justice of the European Union
Claimant May Cite
Ratio
A passenger who was denied boarding because his original flight was cancelled due to extraordinary circumstances and the carrier therefore placed him on a later flight — at which point he was denied boarding due to overbooking of the replacement service — is entitled to Art 7 compensation for the denial of boarding, not the cancellation. The carrier cannot argue that the denial of boarding is absorbed by or extinguished by the earlier extraordinary circumstance affecting the original flight. The Art 7 obligation for denied boarding is separate and distinct from the Art 5 obligation for cancellation.
Key Propositions
  • Denied boarding under Art 4 is a separate, independent right from the Art 5 cancellation regime — extraordinary circumstances on an earlier flight cannot extinguish the denied boarding compensation right.
  • Where a passenger is moved to a replacement flight due to a cancellation and then denied boarding from the replacement flight, the Art 4 denied boarding compensation right is triggered in respect of that replacement flight.
  • The carrier's obligation to pay compensation under Art 7 for denied boarding is not subject to an Art 5(3)-type extraordinary circumstances defence — Art 4 contains no equivalent exemption.
Key Passage
“A passenger denied boarding from a replacement flight offered after the cancellation of their original flight is entitled to compensation under Art 7 read in conjunction with Art 4, even where the original cancellation was due to extraordinary circumstances.”
Key Paragraphs
Paras. 28–41 — denied boarding from a replacement flight is an independent Art 4 right; extraordinary circumstances affecting the original cancelled flight do not extinguish compensation liability for a subsequent denial of boarding on the rebooked service
