## PART A — THE FRAMEWORK

### The two-limb test — Art 5(3) EC261 / UK261

Article 5(3) permits the operating carrier to escape Art 7 compensation where the cancellation or long delay was caused by "extraordinary circumstances which could not have been avoided even if all reasonable measures had been taken". Two limbs, both binding on the carrier:

- **Limb 1** — the event, by its nature or origin, is not inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier concerned;
- **Limb 2** — the event is beyond the actual control of the carrier.

Both must be satisfied cumulatively (*Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia*, C-549/07, 22 Dec 2008). A third, independent requirement operates alongside both limbs: even where the two-limb test is satisfied, the carrier must additionally prove it took *all* reasonable measures to avoid the delay. In practice this is where most defences actually fail.

### Article 9 care — the McDonagh absolute

*Denise McDonagh v Ryanair*, C-12/11 (31 January 2013): Art 9 care obligations (meals, refreshments, hotel accommodation, local transport, communications) are absolute and remain owed by the carrier even where extraordinary circumstances are proved and Art 7 compensation is not payable. No temporal or monetary limit in the current Regulation. This is the "super-extraordinary" principle: even in a volcanic-eruption cancellation, the Art 9 duty survives.

### Sturgeon three-hour trigger

*Sturgeon v Condor and Böck v Air France*, joined cases C-402/07 and C-432/07 (19 November 2009), reaffirmed in *Nelson v Lufthansa and TUI Travel*, C-581/10 and C-629/10 (23 October 2012), and clarified in *Germanwings v Henning*, C-452/13 (4 September 2014) — "arrival" is the moment the cabin door opens, not touchdown. A 3-hour arrival delay at the final destination triggers Art 7 compensation in the same measure as a cancellation.

### The Eglītis buffer duty

*Eglītis and Ratnieks v Latvijas Republikas Ekonomikas Ministrija*, C-294/10 (12 May 2011): the carrier must, at the stage of organising the flight, reasonably take account of the risk of delay from possible extraordinary circumstances, and must provide for a reserve of time to allow it, if possible, to operate the flight once the extraordinary circumstance has ended. This is the doctrinal basis for challenging thin schedules, absent standby crew, and cascading FDP breaches.

### Causal chain integrity — Austrian Airlines and T-656/24

*Austrian Airlines*, C-826/19 (22 April 2021): a carrier may rely on an earlier-rotation extraordinary circumstance only if a sufficiently direct causal link with the delay of the flight in issue is shown.

*NI, HZ v European Air Charter AG*, T-656/24, General Court judgment of 21 January 2026: an airline's voluntary decision to wait for passengers (even a well-intentioned commercial choice) can break the required direct causal link and preclude reliance on the extraordinary-circumstances defence for subsequent rotations. The fact that the airline decision was in passengers' interest is not decisive — it is still an independent, commercial decision by the airline. This is the modern controlling authority on causal chain integrity.

## PART B — DISRUPTION TYPES (15)

### 1. WEATHER — `weather`

**Default position: extraordinary, subject to evidence.** Adverse meteorological conditions incompatible with the safe operation of the flight fall within the Recital 14 non-exhaustive list of extraordinary circumstances. But the position is not automatic — the weather must actually affect *the specific flight in issue*, not merely create difficulties nearby.

**Snowstorm — extraordinary.** *BGH X ZR 136/23* (Bundesgerichtshof, judgment of 24 September 2024) confirmed that a snowstorm at Stuttgart which caused a cancellation qualifies as an extraordinary circumstance. Notably, the BGH also held that the disruptive effects of the snowstorm — already causing delays to previous flights — may be considered for later flights, provided a direct causal link is shown. Airlines may invoke extraordinary circumstances when deciding to cancel particular flights to protect an entire schedule.

**De-icing itself — not extraordinary.** *BGH X ZR 146/23* (2024): the routine de-icing of aircraft in winter operations is inherent to airline activity and not extraordinary. Where a snowstorm causes de-icing queues far beyond expectation, the analysis shifts to the weather event, not the de-icing.

**Evidence required.** METAR / TAF, SIGMET, ATIS, Eurocontrol ATFM regulation notice, commander's report, and — critically — reference to the AFM crosswind/visibility minima that were exceeded. Foreseeability from earlier TAFs undermines the defence.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Thunderstorm at destination airport, ATC-mandated diversion or hold, METAR + TAF corroborate | Defend |
| Winter snowstorm at departure, ATC ground stop, entire schedule affected | Defend (BGH X ZR 136/23) |
| Routine winter de-icing delay, no severe weather | Pay (BGH X ZR 146/23) |
| Foreseeable seasonal fog forecast in TAF issued the day before | Contested — carrier's Eglītis buffer obligation may kick in |

### 2. ATFM / ATC RESTRICTION — `atfm`

**Default position: extraordinary.** Air traffic flow management restrictions, CTOT slot allocations, ground stops and en-route flow control decisions imposed by Eurocontrol Network Manager or a national ATC provider fall within Recital 15 and are treated as third-party decisions beyond the carrier's control.

**Runway closure by third-party event.** *Moens v Ryanair*, C-159/18 (26 June 2019): where a fuel spillage on the runway (not from the carrier's own aircraft) causes the runway to be closed and the flight delayed, the carrier may invoke extraordinary circumstances. The judgment turns on the fact that the fuel did not originate from the claimant carrier's aircraft — if it had, the position would be reversed.

**Evidence required.** Eurocontrol NMOC / NOP log, CTOT assignment reference, IATA delay codes 81-89, OCC log, ATC ground stop notice. Without a documented Eurocontrol reference, the defence is very weak.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| CTOT issued by Eurocontrol due to en-route capacity restriction | Defend |
| Third-party fuel spill closes departure runway (Moens) | Defend |
| Own-aircraft fuel spill closes runway (converse of Moens) | Pay — carrier's own event |
| ATFM regulation but the delay is longer than the slot suggests | Contested — reasonable measures test — could the delay have been shorter? |

### 3. THIRD-PARTY INDUSTRIAL ACTION — `third-party-ia`

**Default position: extraordinary.** Strikes by third-party service providers (ATC, airport security, border control, independent baggage handlers) are recognised as extraordinary circumstances. The European Commission's Your Europe portal expressly lists air traffic management decisions — which includes ATC strikes — as extraordinary. Codified in the EC261 Reform package (agreed 15 June 2026, in force ~2027) as an example on the non-exhaustive list.

**French ATC strikes.** The most litigated third-party IA context. French national law (loi Diard) requires striking ATC to give 48-hour notice, which the DGAC uses to reduce flow. UK County Courts and German Amtsgerichten have consistently treated French ATC strike-caused delays and cancellations as extraordinary, subject to the reasonable-measures test.

**Handler shortage.** *Touristic Aviation Services v Flightright*, C-405/23 (16 May 2024): a shortage of staff at the airport operator responsible for baggage loading may constitute an extraordinary circumstance. But the carrier must proactively seek alternatives — engaging another provider where feasible — to satisfy the reasonable-measures test.

**Evidence required.** Union strike notice, DGAC / national ATC communication, Eurocontrol regulation notice referencing the strike, airport operator notification (for handler shortages), OCC log showing alternatives sought.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| French ATC strike, Eurocontrol regulation on file | Defend |
| Third-party handler shortage, no evidence alternative handler was contacted | Contested (C-405/23) |
| Airport security screening staff strike | Defend |
| Independent ground service provider strike, OCC log shows attempted mitigation | Defend |

### 4. OWN-STAFF INDUSTRIAL ACTION — `own-ia`

**Default position: NOT extraordinary.** *Airhelp v Scandinavian Airlines System*, C-28/20 (23 March 2021): a strike by an airline's own pilots, cabin crew or ground staff — even a strike called by a trade union in the context of collective bargaining — is part of the normal exercise of running an airline and is not extraordinary. Confirms and generalises the earlier position in *Krüsemann v TUIfly*, C-195/17 (17 April 2018), which held that a "wildcat" sick-out following restructuring was still not extraordinary.

**Position is absolute.** Even where the strike is called legitimately under national law, and even where the airline could not lawfully compel staff to work, the CJEU treats the industrial-relations risk as inherent to airline operation.

**Evidence required — for what it is worth.** Union communications, ACAS/DGAC notices, roster records, standby activation logs. But the defence should generally not be run. Airlines lose Art 7 claims on own-staff strikes with near-total consistency.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Pilot strike over pay, called by union with statutory notice | Pay (C-28/20) |
| Wildcat sick-out following restructuring announcement | Pay (Krüsemann C-195/17) |
| Cabin crew industrial action affecting cascading rotations | Pay — extends across all affected flights |

### 5. BIRD STRIKE — `birdstrike`

**Default position: extraordinary.** *Pešková v Travel Service*, C-315/15 (4 May 2017): a collision between an aircraft and a bird — and any damage caused — is not intrinsically linked to the operating system of the aircraft; it is an extraordinary circumstance regardless of whether the collision actually damaged the aircraft.

**Reasonable measures caveat.** The CJEU remitted the reasonable-measures question to the national court. Airlines should take reasonable technical and administrative measures to avoid birdstrikes (e.g. participating in airport wildlife-management programmes, using deterrent systems) but are not required to make "intolerable sacrifices". More importantly, every post-strike delay node must itself be justified: waiting for a second technician to arrive without documented necessity broke the reasonable-measures test in Pešková on the facts.

**Evidence required.** Commander's report, engineering inspection record (nil damage or damage findings), Mandatory Occurrence Report to national aviation authority.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Birdstrike on take-off, precautionary inspection, defence pack complete | Defend |
| Birdstrike + additional operational delay caused by carrier scheduling | Contested — reasonable-measures question |
| Bird ingestion into engine, actual damage, AOG for replacement | Defend — mechanical necessity documented |

### 6. TECHNICAL FAULT — `technical`

**Default position: NOT extraordinary.** *Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia*, C-549/07 (22 December 2008): technical defects discovered during maintenance or arising from lack of maintenance are inherent to airline operations and not extraordinary. *Van der Lans v KLM*, C-257/14 (17 September 2015): the premature failure of a component — even undetected at routine maintenance, and even when the replacement part is unavailable at the airport — is inherent to operations and does not qualify as extraordinary. This is the strongest CJEU statement of the technical-fault rule and is the direct authority behind most airline losses on technical claims.

**Narrow exception: hidden design defect.** *Matkustaja A v Finnair*, C-385/23 (13 June 2024) and *D.S.A. v P.S.A.*, C-411/23 (13 June 2024): where the fault stems from a hidden design or manufacturing defect confirmed by the OEM or by EASA/CAA, and the defect affects the aircraft type generally and implicates flight safety, extraordinary circumstances CAN apply. Timing of the carrier's awareness of the defect is irrelevant (C-411/23) — even a defect known months in advance can be extraordinary, provided the causal link at the moment of disruption holds.

**Additional UK authority.** *Jet2.com v Huzar* [2014] EWCA Civ 791 confirms the Wallentin-Hermann position in England & Wales and is binding on County Courts.

**Evidence required for the design-defect route.** Written OEM statement identifying the defect as a design issue affecting the type; EASA / national CAA airworthiness directive (the gold standard); documentation that the failure implicated flight safety.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Routine engine defect discovered on turn-around | Pay (Wallentin-Hermann / Huzar) |
| Premature component failure, unavailable replacement | Pay (Van der Lans) |
| Fault stems from OEM-confirmed hidden design defect | Defend (C-385/23) |
| Fault covered by active EASA emergency AD on the aircraft type | Defend (C-411/23) |

### 7. FUME EVENT ("smelly-sock") — subset of `technical`

Fully covered in **`specs/defendable_legal_knowledge_fume_events.md`**. Short summary: fume events are technical events under Wallentin-Hermann / Van der Lans and default to *not extraordinary*. The only defensible route is C-385/23 / C-411/23 — proof of a hidden design defect confirmed by OEM or EASA, anchored to an airworthiness directive covering the specific bleed-air component (Boeing 737 5th-stage bleed check valves; EASA Emergency AD 2026-0134 on A320neo HP valve butterfly seal retention clip; A330neo HP valve leaks from August 2022 onwards). Airlines lose more Art 7 claims in this category than in any other technical sub-type because they run "unexpected fault" arguments the Court has expressly rejected. Reasonable measures must include MOR, engineering inspection, timeline justification and Art 9 care. The UK collective action on aerotoxic syndrome (High Court, ~220 crew claimants, lead trials 2025–26) is a personal injury matter and must not be conflated with EC261 defence positioning, but any LOR should be reviewed against that parallel proceeding before sending.

### 8. MEDICAL EMERGENCY / DIVERSION — `medical`

**Default position: extraordinary.** A passenger medical emergency requiring the commander to divert is not inherent in the normal exercise of airline operations and is beyond the carrier's control. The commander's duty to divert is grounded in the Air Navigation Order (UK), ICAO standards, and the airline's own operations manual. No CJEU judgment yet directly on point for a *passenger* medical diversion, but the position follows a fortiori from *LE v Transportes Aéreos Portugueses*, C-74/19 (11 June 2020) — if the unruly conduct of a passenger is extraordinary (see next section), so is a genuine medical emergency requiring the same operational response.

**Reasonable measures caveat — mirrors LE v TAP.** The diversion decision itself is extraordinary; the *timeline afterwards* is not. The carrier must show that once the passenger was disembarked and cared for, the reroute or continuation was executed with reasonable diligence.

**Evidence required.** Commander's report, MedLink (or equivalent physician-on-call service) log, diversion airport ground handling notification, medical crew report at diversion airport, subsequent operational timeline for the affected sector and any cascading sectors.

**Art 9 remains absolute.** Even where the defence succeeds, the McDonagh principle means care obligations continue to run for affected passengers.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Cardiac emergency, commander diverts, evidence pack complete | Defend |
| Passenger medical claim disputed by cabin crew, no MedLink log | Contested |
| Diversion for medical + subsequent sector cancelled for FDP | Defend on the diversion; cascading FDP requires Eglītis buffer analysis |

### 9. SECURITY EVENT — `security`

**Default position: extraordinary where genuine safety event.** Recital 14 expressly includes "security risks" in the non-exhaustive list. Bomb threats, credible airport security incidents, aircraft evacuations following intrusion, and government-imposed airspace restrictions all fall within the defence.

**Screening delays are different.** Routine security-screening delays (queue length, throughput) are treated as inherent to operation — the carrier plans buffer for them. Only unexpected total failure of a security checkpoint (e.g. system outage, mass evacuation) qualifies.

**Causal chain risk — T-656/24 territory.** *NI, HZ v European Air Charter AG*, T-656/24 (General Court judgment of 21 January 2026): a security-related delay at Cologne/Bonn where the airline chose to *wait* for delayed passengers broke the causal chain — the airline's autonomous decision to hold became the determining cause of the subsequent-flight delay, precluding reliance on extraordinary circumstances for those onward flights. The carrier must show that any post-security-event operational choice was operationally required, not a commercial discretion.

**Evidence required.** Police / competent authority incident report, airport security operator notification, aircraft inspection record where relevant, MOR.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Bomb threat, aircraft evacuated and inspected, authority report on file | Defend |
| Airport-wide security event, government-issued restriction | Defend |
| Airline holds for delayed passengers after security event (T-656/24) | Pay — causal chain broken by the airline's decision |
| Routine security screening queue delay | Pay — inherent to operations |

### 10. DISRUPTIVE PASSENGER — `disruptive-pax`

**Default position: extraordinary.** *LE v Transportes Aéreos Portugueses*, C-74/19 (11 June 2020): the unruly behaviour of a passenger which justifies the commander in diverting to disembark is not inherent in the normal exercise of airline activity and is beyond the carrier's control.

**Two important qualifications — read strictly.**

**(a) Not extraordinary if the carrier itself triggered the behaviour** — e.g. denial of service, hostile crew conduct, sales-alcohol contribution. The court will scrutinise contributory factors.

**(b) Rerouting duty is stringent.** The CJEU used LE v TAP to expand the reasonable-measures inquiry: airlines must consider rerouting via *other carriers*, not just their own network. Rebooking on the next available own-metal service without checking third-party alternatives will fail the reasonable-measures test — even where the extraordinary circumstance itself is established.

**Evidence required.** Commander's report, cabin crew statements, police report at diversion airport, offload record, and — critically — documentation of the rerouting search including third-party carrier availability at the time of decision.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Unruly passenger assaults crew, diversion, offload, next-day own-metal rebook checked, no earlier interline available | Defend |
| Unruly passenger diverted, rebook only on next own service, no interline check | Pay — reasonable measures failed (LE v TAP) |
| Passenger disturbance triggered by delayed drinks service | Contested — contributory factor investigation |

### 11. GROUND DAMAGE — `ground-damage`

**Default position: fact-dependent — treat as technical unless third-party origin.** The regulation does not treat all ground damage identically. Where damage to the aircraft (bird ingestion during pushback, hail while parked, apron collision) originates from an external third party — e.g. a ground handler's tug — the defence may run under the C-159/18 Moens analogy (third-party event affecting the aircraft). Where damage arises from the carrier's own operational activity, the Van der Lans position applies and the defence fails.

**Damage caused by an object on the runway.** Where a screw or other FOD lying on the runway damages a tyre, the position aligns with Moens — the debris is not attributable to the carrier's operation and is extraordinary. This has been confirmed in CJEU practice.

**Evidence required.** Airport operator report, ground handler incident report, engineering damage assessment, photographs, and — for third-party origin — the third-party company's admission or airport CCTV.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Ground handler tug strikes aircraft during pushback | Defend — third-party origin |
| Aircraft damaged by FOD on runway (foreign object debris) | Defend (Moens analogy) |
| Aircraft damaged by carrier's own catering vehicle | Pay — carrier's own operation |
| Hail damage while parked on the ramp | Defend — weather / external event |

### 12. AIRPORT / INFRASTRUCTURE CLOSURE — `airport-closure`

**Default position: extraordinary.** Airport closures, runway closures, and infrastructure failures imposed by the airport operator or by a competent authority are events beyond the carrier's control and not inherent to its operation.

**Anchoring authorities.**

- *Moens v Ryanair*, C-159/18 (26 June 2019) — runway closure following third-party fuel spill = extraordinary.
- *SATA International*, C-308/21 (7 July 2022) — a generalised failure of the aircraft refuelling system at the airport = extraordinary. The Court characterised the fuelling system as third-party infrastructure whose failure was outside the carrier's operation.
- *Touristic Aviation Services*, C-405/23 (16 May 2024) — handler staff shortage may be extraordinary but airlines must seek alternatives.

**Reasonable measures caveat.** Even where the closure or infrastructure failure is extraordinary, the carrier must show it acted to mitigate — for example, transferring the aircraft to an alternate stand, requesting priority reopening, or coordinating with the airport operator for expedited resolution.

**Evidence required.** Airport operator formal notice, NOTAM reference, ATC log, engineering report where relevant, OCC log of mitigation attempts.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Airport-wide refuelling system failure (SATA C-308/21) | Defend |
| Runway closed due to third-party fuel spill (Moens C-159/18) | Defend |
| Airport IT outage affecting baggage system, handler alternatives sought (C-405/23) | Defend |
| Airport-mandated closure for safety works notified 3 months in advance | Contested — Eglītis buffer duty may apply |

### 13. NATURAL DISASTER — `natural-disaster`

**Default position: extraordinary.** Volcanic eruption, earthquake, tsunami, extreme weather amounting to a natural disaster all fall within Recital 14 and are treated as extraordinary. *McDonagh v Ryanair*, C-12/11 (31 January 2013) — Icelandic Eyjafjallajökull volcanic ash cloud — expressly treated as extraordinary (and referred to as "super-extraordinary" by the AG opinion).

**Critical rider — Art 9 care survives.** McDonagh's controlling holding is not on Art 7 (which was not disputed) but on Art 9: the airline's obligation to provide care to stranded passengers is absolute and continues throughout the disruption, however extraordinary. Ryanair had failed in that duty during the ash-cloud week and was ordered to pay the passenger's costs.

**Post-reform note.** The 2027 EC261 reform introduces a three-night hotel cap for extraordinary-circumstance cases in the EU. This will not automatically apply under UK261.

**Evidence required.** Government / national civil aviation authority notice, VAAC (Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre) reports for ash-cloud events, meteorological hazard forecasts, NOTAM references.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Volcanic eruption closes affected FIR, VAAC advisory on file | Defend Art 7; Art 9 care remains absolute (McDonagh) |
| Extreme weather amounting to natural disaster (tornado, tsunami surge) | Defend Art 7; Art 9 obligations continue |
| Multi-day disruption with no hotel provision offered | Pay Art 9 costs even where Art 7 defence succeeds |

### 14. POLITICAL UNREST / WAR ZONE — `political-unrest`

**Default position: extraordinary.** Recital 14 explicitly names "political instability" among the extraordinary-circumstances examples. Airspace closures imposed by governments in response to armed conflict, sanctions, or political unrest are quintessentially outside the carrier's control.

**Modern context — Ukraine and Russia.** All Ukrainian civilian airspace has been closed continuously since 24 February 2022 (six FIRs: UKLV, UKBV, UKDV, UKFV, UKOV, plus Kyiv UIR). Reciprocal Russian airspace bans affect ~35 countries. Polish courts have confirmed that circumstances of war release a carrier from the Art 7 obligation for cancellations connected to the Ukraine conflict.

**Sanctions-driven cancellations.** Where a flight is cancelled because a sanctioned entity or route becomes unlawful, the extraordinary-circumstance defence applies straightforwardly — the carrier is legally prohibited from operating.

**Evidence required.** Government airspace closure NOTAM, national aviation authority instruction, EU sanctions register reference, Eurocontrol Conflict Zone Information Bulletin (CZIB) — for Ukraine, EASA CZIB 2022-01 and its revisions.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Flight cancelled because destination airspace is closed by NOTAM | Defend |
| Sanctions render route or aircraft type unlawful to operate | Defend |
| Route diverted around closed airspace, extra flight time causes 3h+ arrival delay | Defend, but the reroute timing must be shown reasonable |
| Political demonstration blocks airport access, government-declared incident | Defend |

### 15. CREW DUTY LIMIT — `crew-fdp`

**Default position: NOT independently extraordinary — depends on the upstream cause.** A crew Flight Duty Period (FDP) breach at the end of a delayed rotation is a symptom, not a cause. The extraordinary-circumstance analysis attaches to the upstream event that caused the delay.

**Rule from Eglītis, applied.** *Eglītis*, C-294/10 (12 May 2011): the carrier must plan reasonable reserve time to allow the flight to be completed even where an extraordinary circumstance intervenes. Where the FDP breach could have been avoided by adequate standby crew or schedule buffer, the reasonable-measures test fails — regardless of whether the upstream event was itself extraordinary.

**Practical position.** If the upstream event (weather, ATC, security) is extraordinary and the FDP breach was an unavoidable consequence given the industry-standard standby cover, the defence extends downstream to the FDP-caused cancellation. If the FDP breach reflects a thin schedule or absent standby, the defence fails.

**Evidence required.** Crew roster records showing duty times, standby crew availability records for the day of disruption, FDP calculation showing the specific breach, evidence of standby activation attempts.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Weather diversion + FDP expired downstream, standby crew activated but unavailable at diversion airport | Defend |
| FDP breach from routine operational delay, no evidence of standby crew planning | Pay |
| ATC ground stop + FDP expiry, industry-standard standby cover in place | Defend |
| Overnight cancellation for FDP with no standby cover at all | Pay — Eglītis buffer duty failed |

### 16. CREW ILLNESS — `crew-sick`

**Default position: NOT extraordinary — binding in UK.** *Lipton v BA CityFlyer* [2024] UKSC 24 (10 July 2024) — the UK Supreme Court held that pilot illness is not an extraordinary circumstance. Pilot illness is inherent to the operations of an air carrier; airlines are expected to properly manage crew planning and pay compensation where cancellations arise from crew illness. This is binding on all UK courts below the UKSC.

**Applies to all crew.** The judgment's reasoning extends to any crew member (flight crew, cabin crew). Lipton is the direct authority against crew-illness defences.

**Retained EU law dimension.** The Supreme Court also used Lipton to clarify the operation of retained EU law post-Brexit: pre-IP-completion-day CJEU rulings continue to bind UK courts at County Court and High Court level; UK Supreme Court and Court of Appeal have limited power to depart. Post-IP-completion CJEU rulings are persuasive only.

**Evidence required — for the very narrow case where the defence might still be run.** Only if crew illness is downstream of another extraordinary event (e.g. crew hospitalised following an in-flight medical or security incident). In that scenario, the defence is really about the upstream event, not the crew illness itself.

**Fact-pattern outcomes.**

| Fact pattern | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Pilot reports sick pre-departure, no replacement available | Pay (Lipton [2024] UKSC 24) |
| Cabin crew shortage due to flu outbreak | Pay — Lipton applies to all crew |
| Crew injured in an inflight security event, downstream cancellation | Defend on the upstream security event, not the crew position |

## PART C — CROSS-CUTTING RULES

### Causal chain (Austrian Airlines + T-656/24)

Applies across every extraordinary-circumstance case where the flight in issue is downstream of an earlier event. Two propositions govern:

- **Cascade permitted with direct causal link.** *Austrian Airlines*, C-826/19: a carrier can rely on an earlier-rotation extraordinary circumstance for a later flight only where a sufficiently direct causal link is shown. The link may run across more than one rotation.
- **Voluntary decision breaks the chain.** *NI, HZ v European Air Charter*, T-656/24 (General Court, 21 January 2026): a carrier's autonomous operational choice — including waiting for delayed passengers, however commercially or humanely reasonable — can constitute the determining cause of the subsequent-flight delay and preclude the defence. The mere fact that the extraordinary event was a necessary condition (sine qua non) is not sufficient.

**Practical implication for the engine.** Every case of a downstream cascade must be tested against T-656/24: was the airline's operational decision after the upstream extraordinary event operationally required, or a commercial discretion? Discretionary decisions that prolonged the delay expose the airline for the downstream flights.

### Re-routing obligations

*LE v Transportes Aéreos Portugueses*, C-74/19: airlines must check third-party carriers as well as own-network options when discharging the Art 8 rerouting duty in the reasonable-measures context. Own-metal-only rebooking without documentation of interline / third-party inquiry fails the test.

*AirHelp v Austrian Airlines*, C-451/20 (2022): the 50% reduction in Art 7 compensation for successful rerouting is assessed against *scheduled* (not actual) arrival time of the rerouting flight.

### Denied boarding

*Finnair v Lassooy*, C-22/11 (4 October 2012): the concept of "denied boarding" extends beyond overbooking to any operational reason — including reallocation of passengers from a previously cancelled flight. Extraordinary circumstances cannot be pleaded as a defence to denied boarding — the Art 4/7 right is absolute where boarding is refused against the passenger's will.

### Refund scope — agency commissions

*Verein für Konsumenteninformation v KLM*, C-45/24 (15 January 2026): where a passenger booked through an intermediary (OTA / travel agent) and the flight is cancelled, the Art 8 refund the carrier must pay includes the commission collected by the intermediary — even where the airline did not know the exact commission amount at the time of booking. Passengers who booked via Opodo, Expedia, Booking.com and similar are entitled to have those commissions refunded. Widespread compliance gap in airline reservation systems.

### EC261 Reform — agreed 15 June 2026, in force ~2027

Political agreement reached in trilogue on 15 June 2026. Key changes (per DLA Piper analysis):

- **Compensation amounts unchanged** (€250/€400/€600 tiers preserved).
- **3-hour threshold preserved** (Sturgeon codified).
- **Proactive contact within 4 days** by the carrier to disrupted passengers.
- **30-day Art 7 response** deadline: acknowledge and pay or refuse with reasons within 30 days of claim.
- **7-day written explanation** on passenger request.
- **Re-routing offer within 3 hours** of original departure; failing that, passenger may self-arrange and be reimbursed up to 400% of ticket price within 14 days.
- **Tarmac delay rule**: after 30 minutes on the ground with doors closed, adequate temperature/toilets/water; at 2 hours, return to gate to disembark.
- **Expanded cancellation definition**: rescheduling ≥1 hour earlier, return to departure airport, and diversions to alternate airports all now treated as cancellations.
- **Sequential ticketing / no-show clause**: airlines cannot deny boarding on a return flight because the passenger did not take the outbound sector.
- **Free personal item (40 × 30 × 15 cm)** must be permitted; advertised fares must include hand baggage.
- **Article 9 hotel cap of 3 nights** for EU-departing extraordinary-circumstance cases. UK261 will not automatically adopt this cap.
