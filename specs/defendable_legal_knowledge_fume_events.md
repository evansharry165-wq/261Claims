# DefendAble — Legal Knowledge Bank
## Fume Events / Smelly-Sock Events — bleed-air contamination
### Deep research note · updated July 2026

---

## THE EVENT

A "fume event" or "smelly-sock event" is an in-flight incident in which contaminated air enters the cabin or cockpit through the bleed-air system. The typical cause is fluid (engine lubricant, hydraulic fluid, anti-icing fluid, or fuel) leaking into the engine bleed-air path — most often via a deteriorating oil seal on an engine bearing, a failing bleed-air valve seal, or a High Pressure (HP) valve butterfly-seal retention clip. ICAO training materials describe the smell as "dirty socks", "smelly feet", "foul", "musty" or "hot lubricant".

Operationally, a fume event may trigger crew donning oxygen, a Pan/Mayday call, precautionary diversion, medical attention on the ground, and a Mandatory Occurrence Report (MOR) to the CAA/EASA. Post-flight, engineering will inspect the bleed-air path, engine oil seals, and cabin air ducting.

---

## WHY THIS AREA IS DIFFICULT FOR AIRLINE DEFENCE

Two forces pull against each other:

1. **The Wallentin-Hermann / Van der Lans line.** The default CJEU treatment of technical faults is that they are inherent to airline operations, no matter how unforeseen. On this doctrine, a fume event caused by seal wear, component failure or leak is straightforwardly a technical event within the carrier's risk sphere — and therefore NOT extraordinary. Article 7 compensation is payable.

2. **The C-385/23 / C-411/23 hidden-design-defect exception.** From June 2024, the CJEU confirmed that where a technical failure stems from an OEM-confirmed hidden design defect affecting the aircraft type and implicating flight safety, extraordinary circumstances CAN apply — and the airline's prior awareness of the defect is irrelevant.

The problem: airlines routinely try to shortcut the analysis by pleading "unexpected technical fault" or "unforeseeable seal failure" without doing the design-defect evidencing work. Those pleas fail under C-257/14. As a result, fume-event Art 7 claims are among the most consistently lost technical claims in reported EU and UK jurisprudence.

## THE CJEU FRAMEWORK APPLIED TO FUME EVENTS

**Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia — C-549/07 (2008).** A technical defect discovered during maintenance is not extraordinary unless it stems from an event not inherent in the normal exercise of the carrier's activity and beyond its actual control. Bearing seal deterioration is inherent — the seal has a design life the carrier is responsible for tracking.

**Van der Lans v KLM — C-257/14 (2015).** Even the premature failure of a component — undetected at routine maintenance, with the replacement part unavailable at the airport — is inherent and does not qualify as extraordinary. This is the direct authority against most fume-event defences: the bleed-air seal or valve is exactly the kind of component that Van der Lans covers.

**Finnair v Matkustaja A — C-385/23 (13 June 2024).** Fuel gauge failure in an aircraft in service ~5 months, subsequently confirmed by the manufacturer as a hidden design defect affecting all aircraft of the type. Held: extraordinary circumstances. The four conditions were: (i) unexpected defect in new-model aircraft; (ii) manufacturer subsequently confirms it as a hidden design defect; (iii) affects all aircraft of that type; (iv) implicates flight safety.

**D.S.A. v P.S.A. — C-411/23 (13 June 2024).** Hidden design defect in engine high-pressure compressor blades. Carrier knew of the defect months before the disruption. Held: extraordinary circumstances. Two important propositions: (a) timing of the carrier's awareness is irrelevant, provided the defect existed at the time of the disruption and was beyond the carrier's control at that moment; (b) the court must assess whether the carrier had reasonable measures available (e.g. dry/wet lease, aircraft swap) — the extraordinary-circumstances finding does not by itself extinguish the reasonable-measures inquiry.

**Read together:** the fume-event defence lives or dies on the design-defect route. If the airline can put evidence before the court that the specific bleed-air component that failed was subject to an OEM-identified or EASA/CAA-identified design defect, the defence has a live chance under C-385/23. If not, Van der Lans forecloses it.

## THE EVIDENCE PACK — WHAT MUST BE ON FILE

For a defensible fume-event position, the case management platform's evidence checklist should demand all of the following:

1. **Aircraft registration and specific component** — which bleed-air valve, seal or duct failed. Serial number where possible.
2. **Engineering post-flight inspection report** — findings on the specific tail, confirming where the contamination entered the bleed-air path.
3. **MOR (mandatory occurrence report)** — filed with the CAA (or national equivalent) within 72 hours. This is a regulatory requirement for endangering fume events and is documentary evidence the carrier did not conceal the event.
4. **OEM confirmation** — a written statement from the airframe or engine manufacturer that the failed component's failure mode stems from an identified design or manufacturing issue affecting the type. Not a service bulletin standing alone (a bulletin identifies a fix, not a cause).
5. **EASA / national-authority reference** — where available, the emergency airworthiness directive (EAD) or airworthiness directive (AD) covering the specific component. This is the decisive evidence under C-385/23 and by far the strongest anchor for the design-defect route.
6. **Timeline of operational decisions** — the crew's return-to-stand, diversion, hold or continue decision, with the safety justification. Any voluntary decision to continue the flight before oxygen was donned needs strong safety rationale (T-656/24 causal-chain risk applies).
7. **Article 9 care record** — refreshments, hotel, transport and medical care where any passenger reported symptoms. Even where extraordinary circumstances succeed on Art 7, Art 9 remains absolute (McDonagh).

## AIRWORTHINESS DIRECTIVES THAT ANCHOR THE DESIGN-DEFECT ROUTE

Recent regulatory actions specifically on bleed-air contamination that make the design-defect route viable:

- **FAA AD on Boeing 737 bleed-air 5th-stage check valves** — corrosion causes valves to stick open, risking compressor stalls and dual-engine power loss. Directly relevant to any 737 fume event traced to that valve.
- **EASA Emergency AD 2026-0134 on Airbus A320neo family HP valve butterfly seal retention clip** — rupture releases foreign object debris into the bleed-air system. Any A320neo fume event where the HPV is implicated should reference this AD.
- **A330neo HP valve leaks (August 2022 onwards)** — Airbus issued OEM notice and EASA followed with successive ADs to replace affected clips and update software.

Where the specific tail is affected by a live AD covering the component that leaked, the airline has an anchor for the C-385/23 route and should defend. Where the tail is not covered by any AD and the fault is bespoke seal wear, the airline should settle rather than run a defence Van der Lans forecloses.

## THE UK COLLECTIVE ACTION — SEPARATE MATTER

A live group litigation in the London High Court, with approximately 220 pilots and cabin crew from British Airways, easyJet, Jet2 and Virgin Atlantic, alleges long-term crew injury from bleed-air contamination — the "aerotoxic syndrome" claim. Lead-claimant trials 2025–26.

This action does not affect EC261 defences on individual passenger claims. It is a personal injury / employer-liability matter. But it materially affects the reputational and disclosure environment: any airline response to a passenger Art 7 claim referencing fume events should be reviewed for consistency with the airline's position in the PI proceedings before it is sent. A Letter of Response that concedes long-term contamination risk, or that overstates the safety of the bleed-air system without supporting engineering data, could be pulled into the collective action's document pool.

## PRACTITIONER'S RULE

Assume a fume-event Art 7 claim is payable unless the file contains, in order:

1. Identification of the specific failed bleed-air component.
2. Written OEM or EASA confirmation that the failure stems from a design or manufacturing defect affecting the aircraft type.
3. A clean reasonable-measures record (MOR filed; engineering inspection documented; operational timeline justified; Art 9 care provided).

Without those three items, the CJEU case law makes the defence unwinnable, and continuing to fight risks costs orders and — worse — creates disclosure that becomes evidence in the parallel aerotoxic PI action. Pay early, log the reasoning, and preserve resources for the cases where the design-defect route is real.
